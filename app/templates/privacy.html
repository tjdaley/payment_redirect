Privacy Matters

Assuring your privacy and maintaining your trust are more to us than collecting data about you. Here's what we gather, what we do with it, and how we protect it.

Data We Gather

Other than what data entered by you through the use of our system, we do not gather much data about you, your business, or your clients.

Data About You

When you sign up, we create an account record with this information in it:

  • id - A unique ID for your account. This allows us to link your account to information you have chosen to save, e.g. a case record.
  • time - The date and time that we created your account record. This helps us track the rate of account creation and plan our capacity accordingly. It would also allow us to archive or remove old unused accounts in the future, although that is not a practice we enage in currently.
  • name - Your fullname, as supplied by the authentication provider and updated by you. You can use any name you want. Some features produce output that makes more sense if you use your real name, but we don't care. It's up to you.
  • email - Your email address. This is your username when logging in to our system. If you use someone else's email address without their permission, we will remove your account once we learn of your rotten behavior.
  • flags - We keeps some flag values that tell us whether you're an administrative user (you're not) or have any other enhanced or deprecated access to our system.

Data About Your Activities

Login History

At this time, we do not maintain a login history or even a "last logged in" timestamp.

Server Logs

Our server logs are rotated very frequently and are NOT archived. We keep some limited log data for a short time to aid in technical support issues. But after 90 minutes (that window is subject to change), we don't have any use for server logs and we delete them on a sliding window basis.

Data We Share

Requests from Third Parties

We do not honor subpoenas from any non-government agency. We just ignore them. If we receive a subpoena from a government agency, and we have any ability and right to do so, we will notify you so that you can hire an attorney and try to resist the subpoena. Our services are hosted on cloud-servers (Amazon AWS, for now) and we can't promise you that they never peek or poke or honor a third-party subpoena or request for instrusion. But that's just life in our modern surveillance state. We'll do all we can to protect you, but we can't promise that anyone else will.

Others

Other than that, we don't share any of your data or any data about you with anyone else.

Data Protection

Passwords

We do not store passwords. We use third-party authentication providers (Microsoft, for now). That means that you must have an active Microsoft account to use our services. Microsoft does not share your password with us or any payment details you have on file with them. We store your name and email address.

Database

Our databases are hosted behind firewalls that do not pass traffic between the Internet and the database. There is no way to connect to our databases from the outside. Even we can't connect to our databases from the outside. So, for example, if one of us loses a laptop in an airport and someone breaks into it, yes, they'll find our query tools, but they will not be able to connect the query tools to the database. It's THAT secure.

Traffic

We support the most recent and strongest HTTPS encryption features. You will use them by default. I suppose you could coax your browser to bypass HTTPS and use an unencrypted HTTP connection, but then, that's on you. We'll assume you had your reasons. As long as you are using the default HTTPS connection, all traffic between your computer and our servers is encrypted and protected from snooping.

NOTE: The only part of an HTTPS connection that is encrypted is the payload. It is exactly like a sealed enveloped deposited in the mail. Anyone can read the address, only the recipient can read the content. (In this example, the envelope contains a letter written in code, so even if someone intercepts and opens the envelope, the contents will make no sense.) Likewise, your internet provider is probably keeping a list of web sites that you visit. So, most likely, is your browser. They don't know what you're doing on our web site and they can't tell what we're sending back to you other than its size.

Remember: We Live in a Surveillance State

We protect all that we can. Just remember, we live in a surveillance state. Governments think it's their business and perhaps in our interest to monitor every move we make, whether in the temporal world or the digital world. We don't disclose anything to them, but they gather up what they can by reading traffic destinations and computer locations. Big Data companies are positioned to make a fortune from whatever they can discern about you so they meticulously track everything they possibley can. Again, they know where you are and they know what web sites you visit. But they have no ability to see what you use our site for or what information we send back to you.
